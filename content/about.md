I read a marketing email until the end. It was worth my time and it left me thinking.

It was about how we always throw our work into the sea... and it may be forgotten or found by someone. If we take the time and care to do work properly, it may take time, and also time to be found. But if it's something precious, worth it, like a carved wooden horse, when it finally washes ashore, someone may pick it up and ask "Who did this?".

At the same time, I was participating in Cohort 7 of [Devrel Uni](https://www.devreluni.com/),where we spent a lot of time discussing what remains valuable as AI changes how we build, write, and create.

In our group sessions, we discussed about taste.

[Hassan](https://x.com/nutlope), one of our mentors, described taste as something that is "learned over time" and which he thinks "you have to have done something a lot and experimented with a lot of stuff to start to get good taste."

That got me thinking.

Having a background in humanities, I can not help but think about my favorist sociologist, Pierre Bordieu. He argued that "taste" was socially produced and created through one's upbringing, education, social class and accumulated cultural capital.

The tension I see and that I see reflected accross articles and social media, and as well in my brain is: Is taste in the AI era a way to gatekeep, just by arguing that something has "taste" or not? Or is "taste" maybe gaining a new definition in this era that separates AI Slop, from the human touch, that paints it with human experience and takes the time to carve the little precious wooden horses?"

This collection is my attemp to explore that tension through examples. To gather wooden horses and ask what made me stop and try to capture them?
